How to Read This Platform
A reference guide to the scoring criteria, hard filters, MSK industry risk table, and color system used throughout the platform.
Hard Filters — Applied Before Scoring
Every company must pass all four filters to appear in the ranked list. Failure on any single filter is an automatic exclusion.
Public Company
Company must be publicly listed or have verifiable public disclosures. Private companies with no public presence are excluded.
Pass / Fail
Workforce Scale
≥50,000 employees globally. Below this threshold, the revenue opportunity at $2 PEPM is insufficient for enterprise sales investment.
Pass / Fail
Global Footprint
Workforce present in ≥2 countries. Sword's Thrive Global product is a multinational solution — single-country companies are out of scope.
Pass / Fail
Geography & Industry
Not headquartered in China. Not an insurance company. Both exclusions are structural — not addressable through the GTM motion.
Pass / Fail
Scoring Criteria — The Five Dimensions
Each company is scored across five criteria. Scores combine into a weighted total that drives account prioritisation.
MSK Exposure Relevance
Weight 20% · Umbrella: Attractiveness / Willingness to Pay · Scored 0–3
How significant is the workforce's physical health and MSK burden? Derived from industry classification using the MSK Industry Risk Reference below.
▾
0
Unverifiable or no information
1
Low MSK exposure — knowledge workforce, primarily sedentary (banking, software, media)
2
Mixed to moderate — mixed workforce with some physical roles (retail, pharma, aviation)
3
High to very high — physical or industrial workforce (logistics, manufacturing, mining, oil & gas)
Employee Headcount
Weight 30% · Umbrella: Added Value · Scored 0–3
How large is the addressable employee population? The single highest-weighted criterion — headcount is the primary revenue driver at $2 PEPM.
▾
0
Below P25 of the dataset — bottom quartile of headcount across all 230 companies
1
P25 – P50 — between the 25th and 50th percentile of the headcount distribution
2
P50 – P75 — between the 50th and 75th percentile of the headcount distribution
3
Above P75 — top quartile of headcount. Thresholds recalibrated empirically from the full 230-company dataset using PERCENTILE.INC, ensuring even distribution across bands.
Benefits Investment Signal (BIS)
Weight 20% · Umbrella: Accessibility / Willingness to Pay · Scored 0–3
How publicly and specifically does the company signal investment in employee wellbeing? Named programs, ESG disclosure depth, and GPTW certification are the key signals.
▾
0
Unverifiable or no information found
1
Generic mention of wellbeing but no specific program in ESG or sustainability report
2
Specific, named wellbeing program documented in ESG or sustainability report
3
Named program AND confirmed Great Place to Work certification at parent entity level
Centralized Benefits Governance (CBG)
Weight 20% · Umbrella: Ease to Close Deal · Scored 0–3
How centralized is the company's benefits decision-making? High CBG = one global buyer with mandate. Low CBG = fragmented, country-by-country structure requiring land-and-expand.
▾
0
Unverifiable or no information — company is private with no public presence
1
Benefits responsibilities appear only in country-specific HR roles — no regional or global scope
2
At least one role combining a regional label with a benefits or total rewards keyword (e.g. EMEA Benefits Manager)
3
At least one director-level or above with a global-scope benefits or total rewards title (e.g. VP Global Benefits)
Existing Digital Benefits Adoption (EDBA)
Weight 10% · Umbrella: Ease of Substitution · Scored –1 / 0 / +1
Does the company already offer digital health benefits? A proxy for benefits tech receptivity, existing vendor relationships, and the selling motion required.
▾
–1
Competitor — A direct Sword Health competitor is installed (Hinge Health, Kaia, Omada for MSK; Lyra, Spring Health, Headspace for MH). Displacement conversation required.
0
Greenfield — No confirmed digital health benefit. Category creation required — educate on MSK/MH as a cost driver before introducing Sword.
+1
Adjacent — Digital health tools in place (EAP, telemedicine, fitness apps) but no direct competitor. Category validated, budget exists — warm entry.

How to Use This Platform
A field guide for sales reps. From first login to closing the deal — how to move through the platform with purpose.
This platform is your pre-call intelligence and deal strategy tool. It does not replace your CRM or your conversations — it prepares you for them. Every company in the list has been scored, researched, and ranked. Your job is to use that work to enter every conversation with a sharper hypothesis than your competition.
01
Find your accounts — Company Explorer
Start here every time. Load your Excel file and use the filters to narrow to the accounts that match your motion. If you run an enterprise direct play, filter to CBG 3 Centralized. If you are building a land-and-expand pipeline, filter to CBG 1–2 Decentralized. Layer MSK to find the right narrative — High MSK for ROI and absenteeism, Low MSK for talent and productivity. Use the search bar to find a specific company by name, industry, or HQ.
The rank reflects research-based prioritisation across five weighted criteria. Use it as your default order, but let your territory and relationships override it where relevant.
02
Open the company playbook — read intelligence first
Click any company to open its assembled playbook. The first section — Company Intelligence — is the only section you need before making your first contact. It tells you who to call (Named Buyer), what wellbeing infrastructure is already in place (BIS), what digital health tools they use (EDB), whether a competitor is installed, and the sourced evidence behind each score. Read this before you touch LinkedIn.
The CBG Source and BIS Source fields link directly to the LinkedIn profiles and ESG reports used in the research. Use them to verify the named buyer is still in role before outreaching.
03
Frame your entry — Competitive Landscape and Positioning
Before crafting your outreach, check the EDB state. Greenfield accounts need category education first — do not lead with product. Adjacent accounts have digital health infrastructure in place; lead with the MSK gap. Competitor accounts require a displacement framing — check whether a battle card is available for the named competitor and use it to anchor your differentiation. Then move to Positioning to find the right headline message and proof points for this specific workforce type.
For EDB –1 accounts with a named competitor, the battle card in §02 shows the competitor's specific vulnerabilities and the three Sword positioning points to lead with. Do not open with a generic pitch.
04
Prepare your messaging — Messaging section
The Messaging section gives you three pre-written statement variants for every account — Standard Opening, Finance-First, and Operations-First for High MSK; Standard, People-First, and Productivity-First for Low MSK. Pick the one that matches your buyer. The pre-written opening at the top is already tailored to the company's EDB state — use it as your first outreach hook or call opener verbatim, then follow with the statement that resonates most with the specific buyer persona you are targeting.
The Finance-First statement is built for the CFO or Procurement conversation. The Operations-First statement is built for CHRO or HR Operations. Match the statement to the person, not the company.
05
Run the right sales motion — GTM Motion & Sales Stages
The GTM Motion section tells you which phase you are in, what exit criteria you need to hit before moving forward, and what questions to ask in discovery. For Centralized accounts, follow the 5-area Discovery Questionnaire before proposing anything. For Decentralized accounts, use the Beachhead Questionnaire to map the local stakeholder landscape and confirm signing authority. The exit criteria for each phase are your deal qualification checklist — if you cannot tick them, you are not ready to advance.
The region selection formula in Phase 2 is not a suggestion — use it to score and rank potential pilot markets before proposing one. It prevents you from anchoring on the most obvious market rather than the highest-probability one.
06
Prepare for the objections you will face — Objection Handling
Before every significant conversation, scan the Objection Handling section. The objections are split by governance model and MSK profile — so a Centralized High MSK account has a different objection set than a Decentralized Low MSK account. Each row tells you what the objection actually signals (what the buyer is really worried about), not just what they say. Prepare your response to the two or three most likely objections before the call. Do not wait until you are in the meeting to think through your answer.
Pay attention to the note rows — they flag reroute signals. If MSK is not a priority for a Decentralized account, the note tells you to pivot to Mind or employer brand framing rather than continuing to push the MSK narrative.
07
For new accounts or strategy sessions — GTM Builder
The GTM Builder is not a per-company tool — it is a template configuration tool. Use it when you are preparing for a new account type not yet in the ranked list, building a strategy for a territory review, or aligning with your manager on the right motion for a segment. Swap the CBG and MSK modules to preview different strategic configurations. The pricing section gives you the exact discount structure and approval thresholds you need for commercial conversations.
The GTM Builder is also useful for onboarding — stepping through the modules with a new team member is the fastest way to walk them through the full Thrive Global GTM logic in one session.
